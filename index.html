Bilingualism

  • The ability to understand and/or communicate in more than one language
  • Is there a minimum requirement to be considered bilingual?

Four components:

read / write / speak / listen

Bilingualism versus Biliteracy

  • Biliteracy: ability to read and write in two languages
  • A bilingual person is not necessarily biliterate
  • Some bilinguals cannot read/write in their two languages
  • Less than half of the world’s languages today are written (SIL International, n.d.)
  • About 12% of the human population is illiterate today (UNESCO, 2025)

Mention that for the vast majority of history, humans did not write. Today, the vast majority of languages remain solely oral.

Linguistic Attitudes & Language Ideologies

Despite the large number of bilinguals in the world, and especially in the United States, people still have very different reactions to bilingualism.

Let’s reflect…

  1. Is one language “better” than another? Why?
  2. Which language sounds more romantic? Where does that idea come from?
  3. Which variety of Spanish is “better”? Mexican? Colombian? Another?
  4. How do young people speak today?
  5. Do you think your parents thought the same way at your age?
  6. What assumptions do we make about how wealthy people speak?
  7. What about people with less money?

discuss with a partner

  • Language attitudes are reactions toward languages, their use in society, and their speakers

  • These attitudes can influence how we speak

  • “Language” here variably refers to…

    • A language (e.g., Spanish)
    • A variety (e.g., Mexican Spanish)
    • A style (e.g., youth speech)
    • A sociolect (e.g., middle-class speech)

  • Attitudes can be:

    • Positive
    • Negative
    • Neutral

Three common ways of evaluating language

  • Correctness → Correct vs. Incorrect

  • Appropriateness → Better vs. Worse

  • Aesthetics → Pleasant vs. Ugly

But…

Who decides what is correct, better, or pleasant?

  • Linguistic Attitudes
    • Evaluations or reactions toward languages
    • Expressed as opinions, beliefs, or prejudices
    • Individual level
  • Language Ideologies
    • Socially constructed ideas about how language should function
    • Linked to political, social, and economic interests
    • Shared across groups or societies
  • Some research
    • Callesano & Carter (2019): perceptions of Spanish varieties
    • Sima Lozano & Be Ramı́rez (2017): pride and identity in Yucatec Maya
    • Purnell et al. (1999): linguistic profiling



“Contemporary raciolinguistic ideologies must be situated within colonial histories that shaped the co-naturalization of language and race as part of the project of modernity” (Rosa & Flores, 2017)

What do colonial processes do?

  • They systematize and classify language and language use:
- Appropriate vs. inappropriate
- Intelligent vs. unintelligent
- e.g.: Grammar of the Castilian Language [@nebrija1492gramatica]

Raciolinguistic ideologies in early colonization

  • Applied to Indigenous populations
  • Language used to justify hierarchies and difference
  • Today: the “white listening subject”

Raciolinguistic ideologies in early colonization

  • Certain ways of speaking are evaluated through white, normative expectations
  • Raciolinguistics today:
    • Stigmatization of Latinx English and Spanish in the U.S.
    • Stigmatization of dialects that have been previously racialized

Language attitudes and ideologies also shape the concept of bilingualism

  • Monoglossic ideologies
    • “One nation, one language”
    • shapes educational policies
    • The reification of the “native speaker”

Who counts as a “native speaker”?

Research shows this figure is often imagined as:

  • White
  • Middle or upper class
  • From specific regions
These assumptions lead to misdiagnosis and misplacement of students in educational systems.

We are often told that being bilingual is an advantage in society

… but an advantage for who?

Zavala & Tupas (2024) argue that bilingualism is not a neutral category, but political

  • In the United States, knowing an indigenous language like Nahuatl has been seen as useless

    • In many countries, indigenous languages are seen as lesser because they do not have a standard grammar or a written form

  • In Peru, “bilingual” is often used with deficit connotations for Quechua-Spanish bilinguals

  • In the Philippines, English-Filipino bilingualism erased the presence of more than 100 local languages.

So we should ask:

  • What counts as bilingual in your context?
  • Only English and Spanish?
  • What about indigenous languages?
Bilingualism is not fixed. It is a diverse and situated experience.

What myths exist about bilingualism?

True or false?

  1. Being bilingual is like being two monolingual people in one body.
  2. You have to have proficient skills in speaking, reading, writing, and listening to be considered bilingual.
  3. Bilingual children can’t achieve/gain the same knowledge that monolingual children can.
  4. Using two languages at once means you aren’t proficient in either.

Being bilingual is like being two monolingual people in one body.

FALSE
  • There is no strict separation between a bilingual’s two languages in their brain
  • It is like a spider web: when you touch a string, the whole web moves

Being bilingual is like being two monolingual people in one body.

  • A bilingual’s two languages are always “on” (Paradis, 1993)

  • This is important when we want to speak and read in one language

Being bilingual is like being two monolingual people in one body.

    • semantics

You have to have proficient skills in speaking, reading, writing, and listening to be considered bilingual.

FALSE

You have to have proficient skills in speaking, reading, writing, and listening to be considered bilingual.

  • Bilingualism comes in all shapes and sizes.

  • People who study dead languages like Latin or Greek might only be able to read.

  • Many languages do not have written forms.

You have to have proficient skills in speaking, reading, writing, and listening to be considered bilingual.

Many people who grow up in the US are exposed to a language other than English at home, and there are many outcomes:

  • can only comprehend spoken language
  • can speak and comprehend, but cannot read or write
  • can read, write, speak, and comprehend spoken language

Bilingual children can’t achieve/gain the same knowledge that monolingual children can.

FALSE

Bilingual children can’t achieve/gain the same knowledge that monolingual children can.

  • Children are evaluated in phases, typically on a standardized scale
    • e.g., by 2 years old, a child should produce at least 200 words
  • Problem: the same scale is normally used to evaluate these two different populations

Bilingual children can’t achieve/gain the same knowledge that monolingual children can.

  • Bilingual children spread their knowledge across two languages
    • They know 200 total words, but only 100 in English
    • They know how to count in English, but not in Spanish
  • This leads to over-diagnosis of developmental delays, when really they may just need a bit more language support at school!

Using two languages at once means you aren’t proficient in either.

FALSE
  • Switching between two languages in a conversation requires proficiency in both languages (Gumperz & Toribio, 1999)

Using two languages at once means you aren’t proficient in either.

  • Bilingual children know what language to speak with who (Harrison & Piette, 1980)
  • Mixing languages is not a sign of confusion or laziness
  • There are rules to mixing languages – it’s not random, and the child knows them!
  • We call this language-mixing codeswitching

La comprensión y la producción de un bilingüe es diferente de la producción y la comprensión de un monolingüe porque los idiomas están siempre encendidos.

The Stroop Test

We use this task to test executive function, which involves…

  • Self control
  • Working memory
  • Mental flexibility

not acting on impulse. holding information in mind. switching between tasks/perspectives.

Executive Function in Bilinguals

  • It’s been found that bilinguals have greater executive function compared to monolinguals (Coderre & Heuven, 2014)
  • This means that bilinguals tend to…
    • have better impulse control
    • hold more information in their mind in the short-term
    • have greater ease switching between tasks

What do you think?

What if my child has Autism or ADHD?

  • For a long time, being bilingual was seen as a disadvantage
  • Parents were told to speak only English at home, and many teachers still suggest this!
  • This is especially the case for parents of children with autism or ADHD

Bilingualism and ADHD

  • People diagnosed with attention deficit disorders tend to have poorer executive function
  • However, bilingualism seems to mitigate the impact when under cognitive duress (Brouillard & Byers-Heinlein, n.d.)

Bilingualism and Autism

  • Autism is an umbrella term for a spectrum of differences from the neurotypical population
  • A typical fear is that a child with autism will be burdened by hearing and speaking two languages
  • However, we have evidence in the contrary!
    • Bilingual children with autism do not show any delays greater than their monolingual counterparts
    • In fact, children with autism from a bilingual environment who are raised as monolingual show detrimental effects!

Beauchamp & MacLeod (2017)

Bilingualism and the Brain

  • Bilingualism makes your brain healthier!
  • There is strong evidence that bilinguals retain more brain matter as they grow older compared to monolinguals (Heim et al., 2019)
  • There is also evidence that bilingualism delays the onset of Alzheimer’s disease! (Woumans et al., 2015)

In sum…

  • Bilingualism does not delay development in typical or atypical populations

  • It is healthy for the brain and provides certain advantage

  • Provides greater executive function

- better short-term memory
- greater multitasking skills
- more self control

How Can we Contribute to Maintaining Bilingualism?

What can you do to maintain your language(s)?

Do activities with your family. Language is social.

What can you do to maintain your language(s)?

  • The language(s) should be spoken whenever possible
    • talk about daily activities
    • tell stories and histories
    • ask questions
    • motivate everyone to participate
USE YOUR LANGAUGE!

What can you do to maintain your language(s)?

Create a network of speakers

  • Look for community activities and people outside your home
  • Don’t be afraid of using different languages at home
  • Interact with others in your target language

What can you do to maintain your language(s)?

  • Tell or read stories in the target language
  • Listening is important too!
  • Promote reading and writing skills by teaching or actively modeling these skills

What can you do to maintain your language(s)?

  • Create safe place for people to communicate in their languages
